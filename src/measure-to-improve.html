          Measures of improvement

          If we, as product managers, are doing our jobs, we strive towards improvement and adding value on a daily basis. But how do we measure whether all our efforts actually lead to results? The digital world is full of metrics that can be of help. There is one however, that stands out: The Net Promotor Score (NPS). It revolves around the following question:

          "How likely is it that you would recommend our company/product/service to a friend or colleague?"

          NPS scores

          Those who respond to this question are asked to give a score anywhere between 0 and 10. The people who respond with a score of 9 or 10 are called 'Promotors'. People who respond with a 0 to 6 are labeled 'Detractors'. And the people who responded with a 7 or 8 are 'Passives'.

          Why NPS

          The tool aims to measure the loyalty that exists between a provider and a consumer. The NPS is seen as an alternative to traditional customer satisfaction research and claims to be correlated with revenue growth. The NPS has been widely adopted by the bigger part of the Fortune 1000 companies, and for good reasons.

          Calculate NPS

          The NPS is calculated by subtracting the % of 'detractors' from the % of 'promotors'. For purposes of calculating a Net Promoter Score, Passives count toward the total number of respondents, thus decreasing the percentage of detractors and promoters and pushing the net score toward 0. It can be very valuable to add an open question to the NPS question to allow the respondent to elaborate on the reasons why they gave their answer. The feedback that is gathered this way can be interesting for a follow-up interview to identify problems and learn more about your users.

          There is much more

          Next to the NPS there are of course many more metrics and statistics that can be used to measure the success of your efforts as a product manager. Before measuring anything, it all starts with having a clear idea of what you want to achieve. In all cases it's important to find the metrics you need to track whether you improve or not. Focus on pursuing your metrics by observing live use and work your numbers in the right direction.

          TL;DR

          The NPS is not the holy grail, but it's a good measure to figure out whether you are building a product that is well received by your users.
